Three Paradigms

① Rule-Based

Explicit musical knowledge encoded as algorithms. Predictable, transparent, but limited by what the programmer knows.

Chord harmonisers · L-systems · Constraint satisfaction

② Learning-Based

Statistical patterns and neural networks learn from data. Can capture subtle style without hand-crafted rules.

Markov chains · RNN/LSTM · Transformers

③ Evolutionary

Populations of melodies "evolve" via selection, crossover and mutation guided by a fitness function.

Genetic algorithms · GenJam · Co-evolution

01

Rule-Based Approaches

Algorithmic Composition & Music Theory as Code

Rule-Based

A Brief History

  • 1957Illiac Suite — Hiller & Isaacson. First computer-composed string quartet using rule-based stochastic methods.
  • 1969HPSCHD — Cage & Hiller. Microtonal algorithmic composition from chance operations.
  • 1978Music for Airports — Brian Eno. Simple tape-loop rules generate ever-changing ambient texture.
  • 1980sIRCAM OpenMusic. Lisp-based constraint satisfaction for contemporary composition.
  • 1994Max/MSP. Visual dataflow patching for real-time rule-based processing.
  • NowLive coding (Sonic Pi, TidalCycles). Rules written as code, executed in real-time on stage.
Emphasise that rule-based systems are still widely used in live performance and interactive installations. They are the foundation that learning-based systems try to improve on.

Types of Rule-Based Systems

Formal Grammars

Context-free or generative grammars describe valid musical phrase structures — like a syntax for melodies.

L-Systems

Recursive string-rewriting rules (Lindenmayer). Map characters to pitches/durations → fractal-like repetition.

Constraint Satisfaction

Specify what must be true (no parallel fifths, voice range, etc.) and let a solver find solutions. Used in choral harmonisation.

Finite State Machines

States = musical contexts (intro, verse, chorus). Transitions governed by rules or randomness.

Markov Chains

Probabilistic transitions between note states. Trained on data → blurs boundary with learning-based approaches.

Expert Systems

Encode a music theorist's knowledge: voice-leading rules, harmonic function, rhythmic templates.

Bach's Voice-Leading Rules

Rules that governed 18th-century counterpoint — and still guide AI music systems today

Forbidden

  • Parallel perfect fifths or octaves
  • Hidden fifths / octaves in outer voices
  • Augmented intervals (melodically)
  • Voice crossing
  • Large leaps without contrary motion

Preferred

  • Step-wise (conjunct) motion
  • Contrary / oblique motion between voices
  • Resolve dissonances by step
  • Leading tone → tonic at cadences
  • Balanced phrase shapes

These rules can be encoded directly in software — or learned from data (Section 2).

02

Learning-Based Approaches

Statistical Models & Neural Networks

Learning

From Rules to Data

Rule-Based

  • Programmer encodes knowledge
  • Every case must be handled
  • Transparent & deterministic
  • Brittle outside defined scope

Learning-Based

  • Model learns from examples
  • Generalises to unseen inputs
  • "Black box" — hard to audit
  • Needs large amounts of data

Key insight: instead of programming "do not use parallel fifths", we show the model thousands of Bach chorales — it discovers the rule itself.

Step 1 — Markov Chains

The simplest statistical music model

A first-order Markov chain says:

"Given the current note, what is the probability distribution over the next note?"
  • Count transitions in a corpus
  • Normalise to probabilities
  • Sample from distribution at generation time
  • Higher-order: condition on last N notes

Example (C major, from Bach)

After G (scale degree 5), likely next notes:

  • A → 30% (stepwise up)
  • F → 25% (stepwise down)
  • E → 15% (third down)
  • C → 20% (tonic — cadence)
  • Other → 10%

Step 2 — Neural Networks

Going deeper than Markov chains

RNN / LSTM

Processes sequences one step at a time. Hidden state carries "memory" of previous context. Excellent for melody and rhythm modelling.

Transformer

Attention mechanism sees the entire sequence at once. State-of-the-art for long-range musical structure (MuseNet, MusicGen).

VAE / GAN

Variational auto-encoders learn a latent space of musical style. Can interpolate between composers or moods.

Diffusion

Learn to reverse a noise process. Increasingly dominant for audio synthesis (Stable Audio, AudioCraft).

Landmark AI Music Systems

  • 2016Google Magenta — TensorFlow toolkit for music & art generation. MelodyRNN, MusicVAE, Piano Genie.
  • 2019OpenAI MuseNet — GPT-2 trained on MIDI. 10 instruments, any style from Mozart to Lady Gaga.
  • 2020OpenAI Jukebox — raw audio with vocals. 1.2M songs. Highly convincing but slow.
  • 2022Riffusion — Stable Diffusion on spectrograms. Seamless interpolation between styles.
  • 2023Meta MusicGen — text/audio-conditioned transformer. Open weights, Apache-2 licence.
  • 2023Google MusicLM — hierarchical sequence-to-sequence model. Story-prompt → 5-minute piece.
Demonstrate online: play.aidemos.com for MuseNet, huggingface.co for MusicGen.

Evolutionary Approaches

Composition by Natural Selection

Evolutionary

Genetic Algorithms in Music

Biological metaphor

  • Chromosome = a melody (sequence of notes)
  • Gene = individual note (pitch + duration)
  • Population = 20–100 candidate melodies
  • Fitness = "how musical is this melody?"
  • Selection = best melodies have more children
  • Crossover = splice two melodies together
  • Mutation = randomly change a note

The cycle

🎲 Random population
↓
📊 Evaluate fitness
↓
🏆 Select best
↓
🧬 Crossover + Mutate
↓ (repeat 50–500 generations)
🎶 Best melody

Designing a Fitness Function

What makes a melody "good"? — the hardest problem in evolutionary music

Objective (rule-based)

  • ✅ Prefer step-wise motion
  • ✅ Pitch range within 1–1.5 octaves
  • ✅ Pitch variety (avoid monotone)
  • ✅ End on tonic or fifth
  • ✅ Arch shape (rise then fall)
  • ❌ Penalise large consecutive leaps

Subjective (interactive EA)

  • 👂 Human rates melodies 1–10
  • 🤖 OR: learned preference model
  • 🎭 GenJam (Biles 1994): audience feedback
  • 💡 Fitness landscape is non-stationary
  • ⚠ Expensive to evaluate

Combined

Mix rule-based fitness with neural-network beauty scorer — best of both worlds.

Historical Evolutionary Systems

  • 1994GenJam (John Biles) — genetic jazz improv system. Audience ratings guide fitness in real-time performance.
  • 1995Evolutionary Music (Horner & Goldberg) — GA applied to four-voice harmonisation.
  • 2001NEUROGEN — evolves neural network weights that produce melodies fitting an aesthetic model.
  • 2007Impro-Visor (Keller et al.) — combines rule-based constraints with evolutionary search for jazz solos.
  • 2018CMA-ES for composition — covariance matrix adaptation for polyphonic music.
  • NowHybrid systems combining GA fitness with neural style embeddings (CLIP-like music critics).
