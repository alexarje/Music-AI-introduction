Rule-Based Approaches

Algorithmic Composition & Music Theory as Code

Rule-Based

A Brief History

  • 1957Illiac Suite — Hiller & Isaacson. First computer-composed string quartet using rule-based stochastic methods.
  • 1969HPSCHD — Cage & Hiller. Microtonal algorithmic composition from chance operations.
  • 1978Music for Airports — Brian Eno. Simple tape-loop rules generate ever-changing ambient texture.
  • 1980sIRCAM OpenMusic. Lisp-based constraint satisfaction for contemporary composition.
  • 1994Max/MSP. Visual dataflow patching for real-time rule-based processing.
  • NowLive coding (Sonic Pi, TidalCycles). Rules written as code, executed in real-time on stage.

🎧 Listen to the Illiac Suite — the 1957 computer quartet still sounds surprisingly modern.

Emphasise that rule-based systems are still widely used in live performance and interactive installations. They are the foundation that learning-based systems try to improve on. Play 30 seconds of the Illiac Suite if there is time.

Bach's Voice-Leading Rules

Rules that governed 18th-century counterpoint — and still guide AI music systems today

Forbidden

  • Parallel perfect fifths or octaves
  • Hidden fifths / octaves in outer voices
  • Augmented intervals (melodically)
  • Voice crossing
  • Large leaps without contrary motion

Preferred

  • Step-wise (conjunct) motion
  • Contrary / oblique motion between voices
  • Resolve dissonances by step
  • Leading tone → tonic at cadences
  • Balanced phrase shapes

These rules can be encoded directly in software — or learned from data (Section 2).

Step 1 — Markov Chains

The simplest statistical music model

"Given the current note, what is the probability of each possible next note?"
  • Count note-to-note transitions in a corpus
  • Generate by rolling the dice at each step
  • Higher-order chains remember the last N notes

How Music Becomes "Text"

Big language-style models read tokens — so we turn notes into a vocabulary.

Same trick as ChatGPT's words: a melody becomes a sentence, and composing becomes predicting the next token.

Inside a Transformer

To predict the next note, attention weighs every previous note — near and far.

Long memory

Can "look back" at a theme from minutes ago.

Music as language

Notes become tokens — same architecture as ChatGPT.

Used by

MuseNet, MusicGen, MusicLM, Suno, Udio.

Bridge from the LSTM demo: the LSTM only carries a compressed memory forward, while attention lets the model directly revisit any earlier note. This is why transformers handle long-range structure.

Sampling & Temperature

The Creativity slider in our demo is temperature — it reshapes the next-note probabilities.

The model never picks one "right" note — it rolls weighted dice. Temperature loads the dice.

Go back to the neural demo and generate twice at T=0.5 and T=1.9 to make this audible.

Inside a Diffusion Model

Generate audio by learning to remove noise, step by step.

Training

Add noise to real music; learn to undo it.

Generation

Start from random noise, denoise ~50 steps.

Used by

Riffusion, Stable Audio, AudioCraft.

Trade-off vs transformers: superb audio fidelity, but many steps → slower, harder in real-time.

Same idea as image diffusion (Stable Diffusion, Midjourney) applied to sound — Riffusion literally runs image diffusion on spectrograms. Mention the steps-vs-speed trade-off.

Evolutionary Approaches

Composition by Natural Selection

Evolutionary

Genetic Algorithms in Music

Biological metaphor

  • Melody = chromosome · note = gene
  • Population = 20–100 candidate melodies
  • Fitness = "how musical is this melody?"
  • Selection — the best get more children
  • Crossover + mutation make the next generation

The cycle

🎲 Random population
↓
📊 Evaluate fitness
↓
🏆 Select best
↓
🧬 Crossover + Mutate
↓ repeat 50–500 generations
🎶 Best melody

Designing a Fitness Function

What makes a melody "good"? — the hardest problem in evolutionary music

Objective (rule-based)

  • ✅ Step-wise motion, arch shape
  • ✅ Stay within ~1.5 octaves
  • ✅ End on tonic or fifth
  • ❌ Penalise large leaps

Subjective (interactive EA)

  • 👂 Humans rate melodies 1–10
  • 🎭 GenJam: live audience feedback
  • ⚠ Slow, and taste keeps shifting

💡 Combined: rules + a learned "beauty scorer" — best of both worlds.

Going Deeper: Evolutionary Systems

  • 1994GenJam (John Biles) — genetic jazz improv; audience ratings guide fitness live.
  • 1995Horner & Goldberg — GA applied to four-voice harmonisation.
  • 2001NEUROGEN — evolves neural-network weights that produce melodies.
  • 2007Impro-Visor — rule-based constraints + evolutionary search for jazz solos.
  • 2018CMA-ES — covariance matrix adaptation for polyphonic music.
  • NowHybrids: GA fitness from neural style embeddings (CLIP-like music critics).

Going Deeper: Beyond Fitness

When "score it and pick the best" isn't enough

🗺 Novelty search

Reward being different instead of "good" — stops every melody converging on the same safe tune.

🏅 Quality–diversity

Keep the best melody per niche (calm, jazzy, chaotic…), not one global winner.

⚔️ Co-evolution

Melodies evolve against critics that also evolve — an arms race. Sound familiar? GANs rediscovered this.

🔗 The loop closes

Modern systems: evolutionary search guided by neural critics, tuned by human feedback — all three paradigms at once.

Which Approach Should You Use?

❓ Must explain every note? Real-time on stage? No data?
→
📐 Rule-based
❓ Lots of examples of the style you want to imitate?
→
🧠 Learning-based
❓ Can you score quality — and want surprises?
→
🧬 Evolutionary

In practice, strong systems mix all three: rules for structure, learning for style, search for surprise.

Ethics: Three Real Cases

🎤 The fake Drake

"Heart on My Sleeve" (2023) — an AI voice-clone track went viral, then was pulled from streaming. Who owns a voice?

⚖️ Labels vs Suno & Udio

Major record labels sued both companies (2024) over training on copyrighted recordings.

🎨 Consent & credit

Most training data was never opted in. How should artists be credited — and paid?
